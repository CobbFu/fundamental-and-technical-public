# Margin of safety

Buy below intrinsic value by a wide enough margin that errors of judgement leave you whole. Originating with Benjamin Graham and inherited by every value-oriented investor in this wiki. The margin absorbs the unavoidable inaccuracy of every estimate — earnings, multiples, terminal value, competitive position — that goes into the calculation of intrinsic value. Without a margin, a wrong estimate is a permanent loss; with one, a wrong estimate is a missed return.

## Warren Buffett

### Partnership era

Across the partnership-era letters (1957–1969) Buffett returned, repeatedly, to the Graham frame:

> The buyer of a security must demand a margin of safety — a meaningful gap between estimated intrinsic value and price paid — to absorb the inevitable errors in estimation that all investors make.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]

For Buffett the margin is not a single calculated number but a *posture*: a refusal to act when the gap is thin, even when the price action invites action. The investor's job is to wait for the wide gap, not to manufacture conviction at the narrow one.

### Berkshire era

Forty-two years after first reading Graham's *The Intelligent Investor*, Buffett used the 1990 letter — written in the wreckage of the junk-bond era — to restate the doctrine in its most compressed form:

> In the final chapter of *The Intelligent Investor* Ben Graham forcefully rejected the dagger thesis: "Confronted with a challenge to distill the secret of sound investment into three words, we venture the motto, Margin of Safety." Forty-two years after reading that, I still think those are the right three words.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1990-berkshire-letter]]

In the same letter Buffett mocked the leveraged-buyout argument that debt focuses managerial attention — comparing it to mounting a dagger on the steering wheel of a car. The image is the margin-of-safety frame applied to capital structure: the roads of business are riddled with potholes; a plan that requires dodging them all is a plan for disaster. The disposition feeds directly into [[topics/position-concentration]] and [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]] — a wide margin justifies size, and Mr. Market's mood supplies the wide margins on the days the investor needs only to be patient.

## Howard Marks

Marks's contribution is to locate the margin of safety *within the cycle* — the margin is wide at certain points in the cycle and narrow at others, and the investor's mistake at the top is precisely accepting a margin that has shrunk:

> [In times of high risk tolerance, investors are] willing to make do with a reduced margin of safety.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII)

And at the symmetric bottom, the structural margin is enormous but is not accepted, because risk aversion has compressed the imagination of what is possible:

> [In times of high risk aversion, investors] find it impossible to identify — even to imagine — investments that offer an adequate margin of safety.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII)

The slammed-shut phase of the credit cycle is what *supplies* the wide margin in the first place — see [[topics/cycles]] for the credit-window framing.
