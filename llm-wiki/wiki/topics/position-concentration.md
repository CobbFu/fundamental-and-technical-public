# Position concentration

A small number of high-conviction positions vs. broad diversification. The concentrated approach assumes the investor has done enough work to *know* which positions are worth concentration, and accepts that errors in conviction will cost more than errors in diversification. Buffett's "20-hole punch card" is the canonical aphorism; the late-Berkshire framing of "The Secret Sauce" — that a handful of winners over a lifetime produce most of the return — is the same argument run forward over 58 years.

## Warren Buffett

### Partnership era

Buffett's partnership letters foreshadowed his later, more famous "20-hole punch card" formulation: an investor with only twenty lifetime investment decisions would, by necessity, make better ones.

> The partnership made few new commitments per year, each well-researched, each sized to materially impact the partnership's returns when right.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]

### 1993 — concentration as risk *reduction*

The 1993 letter is Buffett's most direct attack on the conventional view that diversification is risk reduction. He inverts the relationship — concentration, properly executed, is the *less* risky posture:

> We believe that a policy of portfolio concentration may well *decrease* risk if it raises, as it should, both the intensity with which an investor thinks about a business and the comfort-level he must feel with its economic characteristics before buying into it. In stating this opinion, we define risk, using dictionary terms, as "the possibility of loss or injury."

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]]

The implicit assumption is that depth of work is a substitute for breadth of holdings — and that the academic frame of "risk = volatility" is the wrong frame entirely (see [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]] for the volatility-vs-permanent-loss distinction).

### 2022 — "The Secret Sauce"

Twenty-nine years later, in the 2022 letter, Buffett re-states the same argument as a 58-year empirical fact about Berkshire itself:

> In 58 years of Berkshire management, most of my capital-allocation decisions have been no better than so-so. In some cases, also, bad moves by me have been rescued by very large doses of luck. Our satisfactory results have been the product of about a dozen truly good decisions — that would be about one every five years — and a sometimes-forgotten advantage that favors long-term investors such as Berkshire.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2022-berkshire-letter]]

He closes the section with the asymmetric framing that ties concentration to the *time horizon*:

> The lesson for investors: The weeds wither away in significance as the flowers bloom. Over time, it takes just a few winners to work wonders. And, yes, it helps to start early and live into your 90s as well.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2022-berkshire-letter]]

### 2024 — restated for the next generation

Two years later, in his penultimate letter, Buffett restates the same logic with different cases:

> Our experience is that a single winning decision can make a breathtaking difference over time. (Think GEICO as a business decision, Ajit Jain as a managerial decision and my luck in finding Charlie Munger as a one-of-a-kind partner, personal advisor and steadfast friend.) Mistakes fade away; winners can forever blossom.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2024-berkshire-letter]]

This is, structurally, the same point applied across the three categories that compose Berkshire (businesses, managers, partners): the long-tail distribution of outcomes means concentration on the few right picks dominates everything else. The disposition is the same as on [[topics/margin-of-safety]]: a wide-margin opportunity *deserves* size. Concentration without margin is gambling; margin without concentration leaves return on the table.
