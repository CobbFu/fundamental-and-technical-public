# Owner earnings

Reported earnings adjusted for the cash a business can actually return to its owners without impairing its competitive position. The antidote to GAAP-driven mis-valuation and to the misleading "Cash Flow = (a) + (b)" presentations that became fashionable in 1980s investment-banker pitch books. Buffett formalised the term in the 1986 chairman's letter — the Scott Fetzer Appendix — but the analytical posture is present from the partnership era.

## Warren Buffett

### The 1986 definition (Scott Fetzer Appendix)

The canonical formula:

> If we think through these questions, we can gain some insights about what may be called "owner earnings." These represent (a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges … less (c) the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment, etc. that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume. (If the business requires additional working capital to maintain its competitive position and unit volume, the increment also should be included in (c).)

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]]

Buffett is explicit that (c) — maintenance capex — is a *guess*, but the guess is unavoidable. The discipline is to make the guess rather than ignore it:

> Our owner-earnings equation does not yield the deceptively precise figures provided by GAAP, since (c) must be a guess — and one sometimes very difficult to make. Despite this problem, we consider the owner earnings figure, not the GAAP figure, to be the relevant item for valuation purposes — both for investors in buying stocks and for managers in buying entire businesses. We agree with Keynes's observation: "I would rather be vaguely right than precisely wrong."

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]]

### Why "Cash Flow" alone is dangerous

The 1986 letter takes direct aim at the (a)+(b)-without-(c) "Cash Flow" used by investment bankers to justify leverage:

> Why, then, are "cash flow" numbers so popular today? In answer, we confess our cynicism: we believe these numbers are frequently used by marketers of businesses and securities in attempts to justify the unjustifiable (and thereby to sell what should be the unsalable). … The company or investor believing that the debt-servicing ability or the equity valuation of an enterprise can be measured by totaling (a) and (b) while ignoring (c) is headed for certain trouble.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]]

### Partnership-era proto

Even before the term was coined, the partnership letters showed the disposition:

> Looking past reported GAAP earnings to the cash a business could actually return to its owners without impairing its competitive position. Capital expenditure, working-capital needs, and management's compensation behaviour were already being treated as adjustments to the headline number.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]

Owner earnings is the numerator against which [[topics/margin-of-safety]] is calculated. Use the wrong numerator and the margin is illusory.
