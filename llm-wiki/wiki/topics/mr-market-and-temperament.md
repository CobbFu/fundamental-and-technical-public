# Mr. Market and temperament

Graham's allegory of Mr. Market: a manic-depressive business partner who shows up daily quoting prices, sometimes wildly high and sometimes wildly low. The investor's job is not to *predict* Mr. Market's mood but to *exploit* it — accept his prices when they are attractive, ignore them when they are not. The whole machine depends on temperament: the willingness to do nothing, for long stretches, while the manic partner offers terms that aren't yet good enough.

## Warren Buffett

### Partnership era

Buffett returned to the Graham frame and added an emphasis on temperament as the gating skill — not intellect, not analysis, but the capacity to sit still:

> The investor's job is not to predict Mr. Market's mood but to exploit it: accept his prices when they were attractive, ignore them when they were not. This required the temperament to do nothing for long periods.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]

### Berkshire era

The 1987 chairman's letter contains Buffett's most extended discussion of the Mr. Market allegory — written in the year of the Black Monday crash. Buffett explicitly grounds successful investing in the *temperament* required to ignore the contagion:

> In my opinion, investment success will not be produced by arcane formulae, computer programs or signals flashed by the price behavior of stocks and markets. Rather an investor will succeed by coupling good business judgment with an ability to insulate his thoughts and behavior from the super-contagious emotions that swirl about the marketplace.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1987-berkshire-letter]]

The 1987 letter then states the rule of engagement with Mr. Market in its compressed Berkshire-era form:

> Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1987-berkshire-letter]]

And quotes Graham to lock in the long-run resolution between psychology and value:

> As Ben said: "In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run it is a weighing machine."

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1987-berkshire-letter]] (Buffett quoting Graham)

In the 1986 letter — written near the late-1980s peak — Buffett gives the disposition its most quoted compressed form:

> We simply attempt to be fearful when others are greedy and to be greedy only when others are fearful.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]]

The same disposition supplies the operational rule for cycles: when "little fear is visible in Wall Street" and "euphoria prevails," the buyer's job is to wait; when the pendulum has swung the other way, the buyer's job is to act. See [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]] for the symmetric, late-cycle case.

## Howard Marks

Marks renames the same phenomenon. Where Graham's metaphor is a manic-depressive *partner*, Marks's is a *pendulum*: the mood doesn't randomly swing — it oscillates between psychological extremes, and the swing itself supplies the energy for the eventual reversal:

> The mood swings of the securities markets resemble the movement of a pendulum. Although the midpoint of its arc best describes the location of the pendulum "on average," it actually spends very little of its time there. Instead, it is almost always swinging toward or away from the extremes of its arc. But whenever the pendulum is near either extreme, it is inevitable that it will move back toward the midpoint sooner or later. In fact, it is the movement toward an extreme itself that supplies the energy for the swing back.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VII, originally from Marks's April 1991 memo "First Quarter Performance")

The pendulum swings on multiple axes — *and they all swing together*, which is why bottoms and tops are so extreme:

> First they exhibit high levels of optimism, greed, risk tolerance and credulousness, and their resulting behavior causes asset prices to rise, potential returns to fall, and risk to increase. But then, for some reason — perhaps the arrival of a tipping point — they switch to pessimism, fear, risk aversion and skepticism, and this causes asset prices to fall, prospective returns to rise, and risk to decrease. Notably, each group of phenomena tends to happen in unison, and the swing from one to the other often goes far beyond what reason might call for.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VII)

Marks frames the disposition Buffett describes — *be greedy when others are fearful* — as **contrarianism** at the cycle's extremes, and as a posture that *must* hurt to apply:

> Contrarianism — doing the opposite of what others do, or "leaning against the wind" — is essential for investment success. But as the credit crisis reached a peak last week, people succumbed to the wind rather than resisting. I found very few who were optimistic; most were pessimistic to some degree. Some became genuinely depressed — even a few great investors I know.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII, from Marks's late-2008 memo)

Marks repeatedly quotes Buffett to anchor the prescription:

> The less prudence with which others conduct their affairs, the greater the prudence with which we should conduct our own affairs. *(— Warren Buffett, cited inside Marks's chapter VIII)*

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]]

And the capitulation move — selling the bottom or buying the top because everyone else is — is, for Marks, the most destructive single behavior in cycles:

> There is nothing as disturbing to one's well-being and judgment as to see a friend get rich. *(— Charles Kindleberger, cited by Marks)*

> Eventually they buy the asset well into its rise or sell after it has fallen a great deal. … In other words, after failing to do the right thing in stage one, they compound the error by taking that action in stage three, when it has become the wrong thing to do. That's capitulation.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XII)
