# Wonderful businesses and time

The thesis Buffett evolved through the 1980s — under Munger's pressure — that *business quality dominates entry price* over a long enough horizon. A wonderful business compounds; a mediocre business decays. The investor's job is not primarily to find cheap businesses but to find *durable* businesses at *rational* prices and hold them long enough for the time-arrow to do the work. This is the Buffett-distinct contribution to the Graham-rooted value tradition, made most explicit in the 1989 "Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years" letter. To be augmented by Lynch (the "tenbagger" framing) and by Marks (the "weeds vs flowers" framing — Marks references Buffett directly) as those sources are ingested.

## Warren Buffett

### 1989 — Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years

The single most-quoted Berkshire aphorism comes from the 1989 letter's "Mistakes" section, written after Buffett's first 25 years running Berkshire:

> Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]]

And — from the same section — the inversion of the early-Buffett "cigar butt" disposition into the late-Buffett quality-over-cheapness disposition:

> It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]]

Buffett is explicit that he learned this lesson by *losing* on cigar-butt cheap purchases (Berkshire Hathaway's textile business, Hochschild Kohn, Diversified Retailing) — and learned it slowly, over a period of decades. The 2014 letter would later credit Munger as the partner who forced the shift:

> It took Charlie Munger to break my cigar-butt habits and set the course for building a business that could combine huge size with satisfactory profits.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]]

### Why business quality dominates managerial talent

Also from the 1989 "Mistakes" section, a complementary maxim:

> When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]]

Implication: when business economics are weak, even a great operator merely delays the result; when business economics are strong, even a mediocre operator may produce satisfactory results.

### Moats — the structural source of "wonderful"

In the 2014 letter, Buffett describes the GEICO case study using the term *moat* — the metaphor he and Munger have used since the 1980s for whatever structural feature makes a business durably profitable:

> The company's low costs create a moat — an enduring one — that competitors are unable to cross.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]]

A moat is what makes "time is the friend" true for a business — without it, competition compounds *against* the business at the same rate the business compounds against itself, and the math collapses.

### One-foot hurdles vs seven-foot hurdles

The 1989 letter also articulates the operational consequence of the time-and-quality thesis:

> What we have learned is to avoid them. To the extent we have been successful, it is because we concentrated on identifying one-foot hurdles that we could step over rather than because we acquired any ability to clear seven-footers.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]]

This connects directly to [[topics/circle-of-competence]] — a one-foot hurdle is, by definition, *inside* the circle; a seven-footer is typically *outside* it. The wonderful-business filter is partly a restatement of the circle-of-competence filter, applied to business quality rather than to industry familiarity.
