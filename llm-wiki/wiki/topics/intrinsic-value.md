# Intrinsic value

The figure indicating what a business is *rationally worth* — independent of what the market currently offers for its shares, and (usually) independent of what GAAP book value records as the capital employed in the business. The economic substance the price is meant to track. Both Buffett (across the Berkshire era) and Marks (in *Mastering the Market Cycle* and earlier memos) treat intrinsic value as the anchor without which none of the rest of value investing has meaning: there is no [[topics/margin-of-safety]] without an intrinsic-value estimate to be safe relative to, and there is no decision rule about whether to buy or sell without a price-vs-intrinsic-value comparison.

## Warren Buffett

### The canonical definition (1993)

Buffett's most concise definition:

> Intrinsic value is a present-value estimate of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]]

The definition is structurally a DCF — but the inputs are [[topics/owner-earnings]], not GAAP earnings, and the discount rate Buffett uses is, characteristically, the long-term government bond yield (not a beta-derived CAPM equity cost of capital — see the 1993 letter's parallel attack on academic risk frameworks).

### Intrinsic value vs book value (1989, 1993, 1996)

The 1989 letter — the 25-year-recap letter — opens with the explicit hierarchy: book value is a useful proxy, intrinsic value is the figure that matters.

> What counts, however, is intrinsic value — the figure indicating what all of our constituent businesses are rationally worth. With perfect foresight, this number can be calculated by taking all future cash flows of a business — in and out — and discounting them at prevailing interest rates. So valued, all businesses, from manufacturers of buggy whips to operators of cellular phones, become economic equals.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]]

The 1993 letter restates the distinction in the cleanest form:

> Book value is an accounting term that measures the capital, including retained earnings, that has been put into a business. Intrinsic value is a present-value estimate of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life. At most companies, the two values are unrelated.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]]

By 1996, Buffett had institutionalised the intrinsic-value-tracking discipline at Berkshire by publishing the *two columns* (per-share investments + per-share operating earnings) that he and Munger believed best tracked the unmeasurable intrinsic-value number ([[sources/buffett/letters/1996-berkshire-letter]]).

### Why intrinsic value cannot be precise

Buffett is explicit that the figure is an *estimate*, not a calculation: the (c) maintenance-capex input in the owner-earnings formula is a guess, the cash-flow forecasts are guesses, and reasonable people will derive different numbers from the same business. The discipline is to make the estimate honestly and *with a wide [[topics/margin-of-safety]]*, not to pretend that the estimate is precise.

## Howard Marks

Marks treats intrinsic value as the *first* of the two questions a value investor must answer:

> What do value investors do? They strive to take advantage of discrepancies between "price" and "value." In order to do that successfully, they have to (a) quantify an asset's intrinsic value and how it's likely to change over time and (b) assess how the current market price compares with the asset's intrinsic value, past prices for the asset, the prices of other assets, and "theoretically fair" prices for assets in general.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter I)

The portrait of the disciplined value investor centres on the intrinsic-value calculation as the *unemotional* anchor:

> The superior investor is mature, rational, analytical, objective and unemotional. Thus he performs a thorough analysis of investment fundamentals and the investment environment. He calculates the intrinsic value of each potential investment asset. And he buys when any discount of the price from the current intrinsic value, plus any potential increases in intrinsic value in the future, together suggest that buying at the current price is a good idea.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VII)

Where Marks diverges from Buffett — and where the comparison page (forthcoming) on cycle-positioning matters — is in adding cycle position as a *modifier* of the buy decision: an asset trading at a discount to intrinsic value in a late-cycle environment is a different proposition than the same discount in an early-cycle environment (see [[topics/cycles]]).
