# Circle of competence

The set of businesses an investor genuinely understands well enough to value. Outside the circle, expected returns are zero (or worse) before fees — because outside the circle the investor cannot tell the difference between a business that *deserves* the price the market is offering and one that does not. The discipline is not heroic: it is the simpler practice of *knowing where the boundary is* and refusing to cross it for the sake of looking active. Phase 1 source: Buffett's 1996 "owner's manual" letter. To be augmented by Peter Lynch's "know what you own" framing (*One Up on Wall Street*) when that source is ingested.

## Warren Buffett

### The 1996 owner's manual

Buffett's most-quoted statement of the rule — from the 1996 "owner's manual" section addressed to individual investors building their own portfolios:

> You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1996-berkshire-letter]]

The two halves of the sentence carry equal weight:

- **"The size of that circle is not very important"** — the prescription is *not* "expand your circle." A small circle thoughtfully patrolled produces better returns than a large circle thinly understood. This is the disposition that enables [[topics/position-concentration]]: small circle + deep work = high conviction in a few names, each sized to matter.
- **"Knowing its boundaries, however, is vital."** — most investment errors are not errors *within* the circle; they are errors of having mistakenly believed the circle extended further than it did. The 1996 letter then states the affirmative prescription:

> To invest successfully, you need not understand beta, efficient markets, modern portfolio theory, option pricing or emerging markets. … Your goal as an investor should simply be to purchase, at a rational price, a part interest in an easily-understandable business whose earnings are virtually certain to be materially higher five, ten and twenty years from now.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1996-berkshire-letter]]

Three operational filters embedded:

1. **"Easily-understandable"** — the business model fits inside your head; you can predict the customer's reason to buy and the competitor's reason not to.
2. **"Rational price"** — see [[topics/margin-of-safety]] and [[topics/intrinsic-value]].
3. **"Virtually certain to be materially higher" over a decade-plus horizon** — the high-conviction long horizon is what makes the calculation tractable; trying to forecast next quarter is not the discipline.

### The index-fund recommendation (1993, 1996)

The 1993 letter contains Buffett's famous index-fund recommendation — addressed *precisely* to the investor whose circle of competence does not include picking individual companies:

> By periodically investing in an index fund, for example, the know-nothing investor can actually out-perform most investment professionals. Paradoxically, when "dumb" money acknowledges its limitations, it ceases to be dumb.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]]

This is the *symmetric* application of the rule: an investor outside the circle of competence for individual-company analysis is *not* outside the circle of competence for index investing. The discipline is to know which circle you are in.
