# Drawdowns and temperament

Volatility is the price of admission to long-term returns; temperament is the gating skill. Investors who cannot tolerate drawdowns — both in their own positions and relative to a benchmark — *will* sell at the wrong time, which compresses returns more reliably than any mistake of analysis. The discipline is not heroic conviction; it is the simpler practice of having decided, in advance, what would and would not constitute a thesis break.

## Warren Buffett

### Partnership era — down years are guaranteed

Buffett warned partners that the partnership would have *down years*:

> Any year in which the partnership only matched the Dow's positive return would be an unsatisfactory one for him, even if the absolute number was respectable.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]

Two ideas embedded:

1. **Relative performance is the metric**, not absolute.
2. **Down years are guaranteed.** Investors who pretend otherwise to themselves or to clients are setting up the disposition failure that ends careers.

The widely-cited *"Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1"* in context means "do not buy at prices where a permanent loss of capital is plausible," not "avoid the mark-to-market volatility that comes with owning equities."

### 1986 — the fearful/greedy formula

The most compressed Berkshire-era statement of the disposition:

> Our goal is more modest: we simply attempt to be fearful when others are greedy and to be greedy only when others are fearful.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]]

This is the disposition the cycle *requires* and that almost nobody actually executes — see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]] for the psychological mechanism.

### 1990 — pessimism is your friend, written from Wells Fargo

In the 1990 letter Buffett spelled out the rule that net buyers should *welcome* declining prices — a rule almost universally violated, because the same market that supplies declining prices also supplies the fear that prevents acting on them:

> Declining prices for businesses benefit us, and rising prices hurt us. … We want to do business in such an environment, not because we like pessimism but because we like the prices it produces. It's optimism that is the enemy of the rational buyer.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1990-berkshire-letter]]

This was written while Wells Fargo (Berkshire's then-recent purchase) was being attacked by short-sellers in the early-1990s banking crisis. Buffett's actual response: *celebrate* the decline, because Berkshire was a net buyer — and a net buyer of a position the partnership had already concluded was good is, by definition, getting a better deal.

### 2008 — the financial crisis

The 2008 letter, written from inside the worst credit crisis since the 1930s, restates the same rule in the same words used in 1990 — and explicitly grounds it in Graham's *price-is-what-you-pay-value-is-what-you-get* frame:

> When investing, pessimism is your friend, euphoria the enemy.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2008-berkshire-letter]]

> Long ago, Ben Graham taught me that "Price is what you pay; value is what you get." Whether we're talking about socks or stocks, I like buying quality merchandise when it is marked down.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2008-berkshire-letter]]

The 22-year gap between 1986 ("fearful when others are greedy") and 2008 ("pessimism is your friend, euphoria the enemy") is, in compressed form, the entire argument: the disposition does not change across cycles, eras, or specific market drawdowns. The only thing that changes is whether the investor was prepared in advance to execute it.

## Howard Marks

Marks treats *risk attitude* — not volatility — as the cycling quantity, and inverts the conventional reading of "what makes a market dangerous":

> For me, the bottom line of all of this is that the greatest source of investment risk is the belief that there is no risk. Widespread risk tolerance — or a high degree of investor comfort with risk — is the greatest harbinger of subsequent market declines. But because most investors are following the progression described just above, this is rarely perceived at the time when perceiving it — and turning cautious — is most important.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII)

The symmetry — and the bargain — is at the other end:

> When investors in general are too risk-tolerant, security prices can embody more risk than they do return. When investors are too risk-averse, prices can offer more return than risk.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII)

> Just as risk tolerance is unlimited at the top, it is non-existent at the bottom. This negativity causes prices to fall to levels from which losses are highly unlikely and gains could be enormous. But the sting of the prior declines tends to increase risk aversion and send investors to the sidelines just as prices (and thus risk) are at their lowest.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter VIII)

Marks defines risk explicitly — *not* as the academic's volatility, but as Buffett's permanent loss of capital:

> I lean heavily toward the first definition: in my view, risk is primarily the likelihood of permanent capital loss. But there's also such a thing as opportunity risk: the likelihood of missing out on potential gains. Put the two together and we see that risk is the possibility of things not going the way we want.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter I)

The temperament implication: at the top, the disposition to tolerate the *missed-gains* drawdown of staying defensive; at the bottom, the disposition to tolerate the *mark-to-market* drawdown of having bought too early. Both are unforced errors only the second-level thinker avoids — and the system is structured so that the average investor will fail both tests.
