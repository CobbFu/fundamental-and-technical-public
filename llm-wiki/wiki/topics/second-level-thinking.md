# Second-level thinking

The discipline of pricing not the *consensus expectation* about an asset, but the *divergence* between consensus and what is likely to actually happen. First-level thinking says "this is a good company, so I should buy." Second-level thinking asks, "Is the market's view of this company too pessimistic, too optimistic, or already right?" — and trades only when the market's price embodies a view that the second-level thinker has reason to believe is wrong.

The point is structural: in any market, the *price* already reflects the average view of the average investor. To outperform the average, your view must differ from the average view *and* be more correct than the average view. Being right about the company while being right in the same way as everyone else gives no edge. Being differently wrong gives a negative edge. Only "differently and more correctly" is alpha.

## Howard Marks

The term is Marks's signature. He develops it at length in *The Most Important Thing* (2011) and returns to it in *Mastering the Market Cycle* (2018) as the source of asymmetrical performance:

**The definition:**

> The essential ingredient — superior insight into intrinsic value — is what gives them that ability. I call it "second-level thinking": the ability to think differently from the consensus and better.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XVI)

**A worked example.** *BusinessWeek* ran "The Death of Equities" on its August 1979 cover. The first-level reading of "stocks have done badly for years and are unloved" is "stocks will do badly going forward." The second-level reading inverts this:

> It takes a highly simplistic first-level thinker to conclude that poor past performance has led to unpopularity today, which implies poor performance tomorrow. Rather, the insightful second-level thinker says poor past performance has led to unpopularity today, which implies low prices today, which in turn implies good performance tomorrow.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XVI)

**Why most can't do it.** The market price is by construction the consensus, set by buyers who think it's a smart purchase and sellers who think it's fully priced. Theory (efficient markets), logic (averages can't beat averages), and the empirical record (most active managers lose to indexes after costs) all converge on the same conclusion — most participants cannot outperform. The few who do consistently are doing something the others aren't:

> Lots of people do so every year, but usually no more than would be the case under an assumption of randomness. A few do it more consistently than randomness would suggest, and some of those become famous.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XVI)

**The hallmark is asymmetry — not higher return per se, but a better ratio of winners to losers and a better magnitude of winners vs losers:**

> An investor with negative skill at selection picks proportionally more losers than winners and thus does worse than the market, both when it goes up and when it goes down…
>
> But the skillful selector has a better ratio of winners to losers than the market; he is able to invest more in his winners than his losers; and his winners are more successful than his losers are unsuccessful.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XVI)

**Cycle positioning is itself an act of second-level thinking.** When the consensus is "things have been going well, they'll keep going well, risk is low," the second-level thinker prices the divergence between that consensus and the historical pattern (cycles do not stay at the midpoint; risk is highest when risk tolerance is highest).
