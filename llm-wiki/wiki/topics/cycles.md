# Cycles

Economies, profits, credit availability, investor psychology, and market prices all move in *cycles* — not random walks and not linear trends, but oscillations around a midpoint that the system rarely occupies for long. The investor's task is not to forecast where the cycle is going (the macro future is largely unknowable) but to *recognise where we now stand* in it, and to *calibrate* portfolio aggressiveness vs. defensiveness accordingly. Knowing the position of the cycle tilts the probability distribution governing future returns — it does not produce certainty about any single outcome.

The cycles that matter (per Marks) are interlocking, not independent: the economic cycle drives the profit cycle, profits drive psychology, psychology drives risk attitudes, risk attitudes drive the credit cycle, and the credit cycle in turn drives the next round of the economy. Excess in one cycle tends to push the others to excess, and the reversal — when it comes — is usually triggered not by an external shock but by the unsustainability of the excess itself.

## Howard Marks

The book-length statement of the framework is Marks's *Mastering the Market Cycle* (2018), an extension of his 1990–present client memos. Marks's structural claims:

**The midpoint is exceptional, not normal.** The cycle's midpoint is what is usually called "the secular trend" or "normal" — and the system spends very little time there:

> The cycle oscillates… around the midpoint. The midpoint of a cycle is generally thought of as the secular trend, norm, mean, average or "happy medium," and generally as being in some sense "right and proper." The extremes of the cycle, on the other hand, are thought of as aberrations or excesses to be returned from… While the thing that's cycling tends to spend much of the time above or below it, eventual movement back in the direction of the mean is usually the rule. … But it usually doesn't stay at normal for long, as the influences responsible for the swing toward the midpoint invariably continue in force and thus cause the swing back from an extreme to proceed through the midpoint and then carry further, toward the opposite extreme.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter II)

**The motion is self-reinforcing.** Extremes don't reverse because someone calls a top — they reverse because the conditions that produced the extreme exhaust themselves:

> As the phenomenon swings toward an extreme, this movement gives it energy, which it stores. Eventually its increased weight makes it harder for the swing to continue further from the midpoint, and it reaches a maximum beyond which it can no longer proceed.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter II)

**Cycles are inevitable; "this time it's different" is the warning siren, not the disclaimer.**

> Cycles are inevitable. Every once in a while, an up- or down-leg goes on for a long time and/or to a great extreme, and people start to say "this time it's different." They cite the changes in geopolitics, institutions, technology or behavior that have rendered the "old rules" obsolete. They make investment decisions that extrapolate the recent trend. But then it usually turns out that the old rules do still apply, and the cycle resumes. In the end, trees don't grow to the sky, and few things go to zero. Rather, most phenomena turn out to be cyclical.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter II)

**The credit cycle is the most volatile and the most impactful.** This is where the bargains come from:

> Superior investing doesn't come from buying high-quality assets, but from buying when the deal is good, the price is low, the potential return is substantial, and the risk is limited. These conditions are much more the case when the credit markets are in the less-euphoric, more-stringent part of their cycle. The slammed-shut phase of the credit cycle probably does more to make bargains available than any other single factor.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter IX, citing Marks's December 2010 memo "Open and Shut")

> Whereas economies fluctuate a little and profits a fair bit, the credit window opens wide and then slams shut.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter IX)

**Three stages of a bull market** (a Bob Farrell formulation Marks adopts and credits):

1. A few unusually perceptive people believe things will get better.
2. Most investors realize that improvement is taking place.
3. Everyone concludes things will get better forever.

> What the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end.

> First the innovator, then the imitator, then the idiot. *(— Warren Buffett, cited inside Marks's chapter XII)*

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XII, "Putting It All Together — The Market Cycle")

**The investor's response is calibration, not all-in / all-out.** Cycle positioning is the process of deciding the risk posture of the portfolio in response to where you judge the cycle to stand:

> Cycle positioning primarily consists of choosing between aggressiveness and defensiveness: increasing and decreasing exposure to market movements. The recipe for success here consists of (a) thoughtful analysis of where the market stands in its cycle, (b) a resulting increase in aggressiveness or defensiveness, and (c) being proved right.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XVIII)

> The key word is "calibrate." The amount you have invested, your allocation of capital among the various possibilities, and the riskiness of the things you own all should be calibrated along a continuum that runs from aggressive to defensive. … When we're getting value cheap, we should be aggressive; when we're getting value expensive, we should pull back.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter I, citing Marks's September 2017 memo "Yet Again?")

**Don't expect this to work in the middle.** The dependable opportunity is at the extremes, not in the comfortable zone:

> Importantly, I want to call attention to the obvious fact that — rather than the everyday ups and downs of the market — the clear examples provided in this book all concern "once-in-a-lifetime" cyclical extremes (which these days seem to happen about once a decade)… Detecting and exploiting the extremes is really the best we can hope for. And I believe it can be done dependably — if you're analytical, insightful, experienced (or well-versed in history) and unemotional. That means, however, that you shouldn't expect to reach profitable conclusions daily, monthly or even yearly.

— [[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] (Chapter XV)

## Warren Buffett

Buffett rarely uses the word "cycle" — his cyclical thinking is expressed through Mr. Market psychology (see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]]) and the fearful/greedy disposition (see [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]]). The 2000 letter — written at the dot-com peak — does, however, distil the late-cycle dynamic into one of the most quoted aphorisms in the Berkshire corpus:

> But a pin lies in wait for every bubble. And when the two eventually meet, a new wave of investors learns some very old lessons: First, many in Wall Street — a community in which quality control is not prized — will sell investors anything they will buy. Second, speculation is most dangerous when it looks easiest.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2000-berkshire-letter]]

Same disposition as Marks's "what the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end" — restated as the operating warning at the top of a specific cycle, with two specific lessons attached. The Buffett-Marks agreement on this is one of the cleanest convergences in the wiki — see comparison page (forthcoming) on cycle-positioning.
