# Berkshire Hathaway

The holding company Warren Buffett took control of in 1965 and converted, over the following six decades, from a structurally challenged New England textile manufacturer into the largest non-financial conglomerate in the world. Berkshire is the canonical case study of the Buffett–Munger application of value-investing principles at scale, and the vehicle through which most of the Berkshire-era source material in this wiki was authored. Forty-eight annual chairman's letters (1977–2024) are ingested as `[[sources/buffett/letters/]]` — see [[authors/buffett]] for the per-letter index.

## Origins (1839–1965)

The Berkshire Cotton Manufacturing Company was incorporated in Massachusetts in 1839. Through a long sequence of mergers, by the mid-1960s "Berkshire Hathaway Inc." was a textile manufacturer of declining competitive position — high capital intensity, falling unit demand, low returns on invested capital. Buffett Partnership Ltd. began buying the shares as a "general" (a Graham-style cheap statistical bargain) — see [[topics/margin-of-safety]] in its partnership-era form.

The takeover in 1965 — provoked by a CEO who had offered to tender his shares at a higher price than was eventually delivered — Buffett would later openly describe as an emotional rather than analytical decision ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]]). The lesson he drew was that *pique* should never drive a capital allocation decision.

## The textile shutdown (1985)

By the mid-1980s, the textile mill's returns no longer justified continued operation. The 1985 letter contains Buffett's most candid reflection on the entire decision:

> When Buffett Partnership, Ltd., an investment partnership of which I was general partner, bought control of Berkshire Hathaway 21 years ago, it had an accounting net worth of $22 million, all devoted to the textile business. The company's intrinsic business value, however, was considerably less because the textile assets were unable to earn returns commensurate with their accounting value.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1985-berkshire-letter]]

The closure of the textile operation in 1985 closes Act 1 of the Berkshire story — the partnership-era cigar-butt thesis applied to one of its largest holdings, with the textile business serving (in retrospect) as Buffett's most-quoted example against himself.

## The insurance float story (1967–present)

The economic substance of Berkshire is the *insurance float* — premiums collected by the insurance subsidiaries before claims are paid, held and invested by Berkshire in the interim. Float arrived at Berkshire in 1967 with the acquisition of National Indemnity. The 1996 letter contains Buffett's most-cited explanation of what float is and why it matters:

> To begin with, float is money we hold but don't own. In an insurance operation, float arises because premiums are received before losses are paid. … An insurance business has value if its cost of float over time is less than the cost the company would otherwise incur to obtain funds. But the business is an albatross if the cost of its float is higher than market rates for money.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/1996-berkshire-letter]]

Berkshire's insurance subsidiaries — National Indemnity, GEICO (full acquisition 1996), General Re (1998), Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group, and the speciality / primary lines — have consistently produced float at a *negative cost* (underwriting profit), making the float a uniquely cheap source of investment capital and a major component of Berkshire's intrinsic value that is *not* reflected in book value.

## The See's Candies inflection (1972)

The 1972 acquisition of See's Candies — at a multiple Buffett described as the highest he had paid up to that point — was the moment the cigar-butt orthodoxy began to give way to the wonderful-business-at-fair-price discipline. See's was small in absolute terms but, in Buffett's later telling, was the case that revealed what a true consumer franchise — predictable pricing power, low capital intensity, durable unit growth — was actually worth.

The 1989 letter's "Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years" articulates the shift explicitly; the 2014 letter credits Munger as the partner who forced it:

> It took Charlie Munger to break my cigar-butt habits and set the course for building a business that could combine huge size with satisfactory profits.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]]

See's still operates as a Berkshire subsidiary fifty-plus years later and is referenced across the corpus as the prototype of the wonderful-business framing. See [[topics/wonderful-businesses-and-time]].

## Key public-market positions

The Berkshire-era letters narrate the major public-market positions in real time:

- **Capital Cities / ABC** — featured 1985–1996, the acquisition of Capital Cities by Disney in 1996 yielded a multibillion-dollar gain
- **The Washington Post Company** — held from 1973, a Graham-era purchase Buffett held for forty years
- **Coca-Cola** — purchase started 1988, the seven-year accumulation of 400 million shares completed in 1994 at a total cost of $1.3 billion. The 2022 letter ("The Secret Sauce") uses Coke as one of the canonical "few decisions" examples: by 2022 the annual dividend alone was $704 million ([[sources/buffett/letters/2022-berkshire-letter]])
- **GEICO** — minority stake held since the 1970s, full acquisition completed 1996, repeatedly referenced as the proto-moat case study ("The company's low costs create a moat — an enduring one — that competitors are unable to cross" — [[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]])
- **American Express** — held continuously since the 1990s, the parallel case to Coke in the 2022 "Secret Sauce" framing
- **Burlington Northern** — full acquisition 2009, the largest acquisition in Berkshire history to that point
- **Apple** — purchase started 2016, by 2020s the largest single equity position
- **Japanese trading houses** — initial stakes 2020, expanded through the 2024 letter

## The Buffett–Munger partnership

Charlie Munger joined as Vice Chairman in 1978 (formally) but had been informally a partner since the late 1950s, through the Wesco / Blue Chip Stamps period. The 2023 letter — written after Munger's death in November 2023 — contains Buffett's most-quoted statement on the partnership:

> In reality, Charlie was the "architect" of the present Berkshire, and I acted as the "general contractor" to carry out the day-by-day construction of his vision. Charlie never sought to take credit for his role as creator but instead let me take the bows and receive the accolades.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2023-berkshire-letter]]

The 2014 letter contains Munger's own separate section reflecting on the partnership and on what made Berkshire work — including his observation that "Buffett's decision to limit his activities to a few kinds and to maximize his attention to them, and to keep doing so for 50 years, was a lollapalooza" ([[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]]).

## Six decades — the structural facts

By 2024:

- Per-share book value compounded from $19 (1964) to ~$148,000+ over six decades
- Total market capitalisation over $1 trillion
- ~390,000 employees across roughly seventy directly held operating subsidiaries
- Equity portfolio dominated by Apple, Bank of America, American Express, Coca-Cola, Chevron, Kraft Heinz, and the Japanese trading houses
- Cash and Treasury bill holdings consistently above $100 billion in the 2020s

The 2014 letter — the 50-year recap — gives the headline figures with Buffett's characteristic understatement:

> Per-share book value has grown from $19 to $146,186, a rate of 19.4% compounded annually.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2014-berkshire-letter]]

## Built to last — the institutional design

The 2023 letter contains Buffett's clearest statement of the institutional design intent:

> Berkshire is built to last. Never risk permanent loss of capital. Thanks to the American tailwind and the power of compound interest, the arena in which we operate has been — and will be — rewarding if you make a couple of good decisions during a lifetime and avoid serious mistakes.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2023-berkshire-letter]]

The 2024 letter formalises the Greg Abel succession:

> At 94, it won't be long before Greg Abel replaces me as CEO and will be writing the annual letters. Greg shares the Berkshire creed that a "report" is what a Berkshire CEO annually owes to owners.

— [[sources/buffett/letters/2024-berkshire-letter]]
