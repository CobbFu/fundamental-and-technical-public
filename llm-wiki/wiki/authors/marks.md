# Howard Marks

Howard S. Marks (b. 1946, New York) — co-founder and co-chairman of Oaktree Capital Management (founded 1995), Citicorp portfolio manager (1969–1985), TCW Group (1985–1995). The dominant philosopher of credit-market and distressed-debt cycles in the modern era. Best known for the Oaktree client memos (1990–present) — a corpus Warren Buffett has publicly said he reads "the moment they arrive." Two books synthesise the memos: *The Most Important Thing* (2011) and *Mastering the Market Cycle* (2018). Marks's frame is downside-aware contrarianism applied to where-we-stand-in-the-cycle judgements — not market timing in the technical sense, but probability-distribution shifting against the prevailing mood.

## Core principles

- **Cycles, not forecasts.** The macro future is largely unknowable; the position of the cycle is observable and decision-relevant. Knowing where we stand tilts the probability distribution governing future outcomes, even when the specific outcome remains unknowable. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/cycles]])
- **The pendulum of investor psychology.** Markets oscillate not around fundamentals but around mood — greed/fear, risk tolerance/risk aversion, credulousness/skepticism. The pendulum spends very little time at the midpoint; it is almost always swinging toward or away from an extreme, and the swing itself supplies the energy for the eventual reversal. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]])
- **Second-level thinking.** Outperformance requires not just a view but a *different* and *more correct* view than the consensus already embedded in price. First-level thinking says "good company, buy it"; second-level thinking asks "is the market's view of this company too pessimistic, too optimistic, or already right?" ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/second-level-thinking]])
- **The greatest source of investment risk is the belief that there is no risk.** Widespread risk tolerance — a high degree of investor comfort with risk — is the greatest harbinger of subsequent market declines. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]])
- **The credit window opens wide and slams shut.** The credit cycle is the most volatile of the cycles and has the greatest impact; small fluctuations in the economy produce large fluctuations in the availability of credit. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/cycles]])
- **Calibrate, don't predict.** The right portfolio response to cycle position is to adjust the aggressiveness/defensiveness dial along a continuum — not to be all-in or all-out. "When we're getting value cheap, we should be aggressive; when we're getting value expensive, we should pull back." ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]])
- **Contrarianism is essential but hard.** When others fail to worry, we must turn cautious; when others are panicked, we must turn aggressive. The signal is psychological extremity, not chart pattern. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]] — see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]])
- **Asymmetry is the mark of skill.** The superior investor has upside disproportionate to their downside — not a higher absolute return, but a better ratio of winners to losers and a better magnitude of winners vs losers. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]])
- **You don't have to catch a falling knife — but by the time the dust has settled, the bargains will be gone.** Waiting for full clarity at the bottom forfeits the opportunity that exists *because* of the uncertainty. ([[sources/marks/book-chapters/2018-mastering-the-market-cycle]])

## Characteristic mental models

- **The pendulum.** Mood swings from one psychological extreme to another, never resting at the midpoint. Greed/fear, risk tolerance/aversion, optimism/pessimism, credulousness/skepticism — all swing in unison.
- **The credit window** — open wide or slammed shut, with little time spent in between. The slammed-shut phase is where bargains are made.
- **"What the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end."** Marks's favoured 14-word distillation of cycle psychology, mirrored by Buffett's "first the innovator, then the imitator, then the idiot."
- **Three stages of a bull market:** (1) a few unusually perceptive people believe things will get better; (2) most investors realize improvement is taking place; (3) everyone concludes things will get better forever.
- **Three stages of a bear market:** (1) a few thoughtful investors recognize trouble; (2) most investors recognize deterioration; (3) everyone is convinced things can only get worse.
- **Cycle positioning vs asset selection** — the two distinct skills in portfolio management; most investors have neither, the great ones have both.

## Recurring failure modes he warns against

- Treating risk as volatility (academic definition) rather than as probability of permanent capital loss
- Believing "this time it's different" — the four most expensive words in investing
- Capitulation — selling at the bottom because everyone else is selling, buying at the top because everyone else is buying
- Reaching for yield in benign periods (when risk premia compress, expected return falls *below* expected risk)
- Confusing safety with comfort — buying high-quality assets at any price vs. buying when the deal is good
- Macro forecasting as the basis for outperformance — Marks: "very few investors are known for having outperformed through macro forecasting"
- Refusing to catch any falling knife — by the time clarity arrives, the bargains have already been bought
- The "extreme brevity of the financial memory" (Galbraith, quoted by Marks) — forgetting the last cycle's lessons just in time for the next one
