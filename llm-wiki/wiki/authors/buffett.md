# Warren Buffett

Warren E. Buffett (b. 1930, Omaha, Nebraska) — student of Benjamin Graham at Columbia, founder of Buffett Partnership Ltd. (1956–1969), Chairman & CEO of Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (1965–present, with Greg Abel announced as 2025 successor). The most-quoted investor of the 20th century and a primary lineage-bearer of the Graham value-investing tradition. The partnership-era letters (1957–1969) and the Berkshire chairman's letters (1977–2024) together form the most substantial primary-source corpus of any single investor in this wiki — Berkshire-era material in this wiki spans 48 annual letters from 1977 through 2024.

## The two-act arc

Buffett's investing thought is most usefully read as a two-act progression rather than a single static doctrine:

**Act 1 — Graham orthodoxy (~1956–1969, partnership era).** Buy *statistically cheap* securities — net-nets, undervalued generals, workouts, controls. The valuation discipline is Graham's: insist on a wide [[topics/margin-of-safety]] between price and conservative intrinsic-value estimate. Business quality is secondary to price. Berkshire Hathaway itself — a structurally challenged textile manufacturer — was a Phase-1 acquisition under this disposition, a decision Buffett would later openly regret.

**Act 2 — Munger-shifted "wonderful business at fair price" (~1972–present, Berkshire era).** Under Munger's pressure, Buffett shifts from buying cheap businesses to buying *good* businesses at rational prices. The pivotal 1972 acquisition: See's Candies. The 1989 letter ("Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years") is the explicit articulation of the shift; the 2014 letter ("It took Charlie Munger to break my cigar-butt habits…") names Munger as the cause. Quality of business dominates entry price over a long enough horizon — see [[topics/wonderful-businesses-and-time]].

The shift between acts is gradual, not binary. Margin of safety remains load-bearing through the Berkshire era ([[sources/buffett/letters/1990-berkshire-letter]] — "three words: Margin of Safety"). The change is in *what is being valued*: Phase 1, the price–book discount; Phase 2, the durability of [[topics/owner-earnings]] over decades.

## Core principles

### Foundations from the partnership era

- **Margin of safety as posture, not formula.** The willingness to sit still until the price gap is wide. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]] — see [[topics/margin-of-safety]])
- **Temperament over intellect.** The most important quality for an investor. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]] — see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]])
- **Concentration when conviction is real.** The "20-hole punch card" disposition — few decisions, each material. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]] — see [[topics/position-concentration]])
- **Relative performance is the long-run measure.** Beating the benchmark by a meaningful margin over a long enough span. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]] — see [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]])

### Refinements through the Berkshire era

- **Owner earnings, formalised.** The 1986 Scott Fetzer Appendix gave the canonical definition: GAAP earnings + non-cash charges − maintenance capex. The figure cannot be precise, but the discipline is to make the guess rather than ignore it. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/owner-earnings]])
- **Mr. Market is to be exploited, not consulted.** "Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful." ([[sources/buffett/letters/1987-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]])
- **Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre.** Business quality dominates entry price over a long enough horizon. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/wonderful-businesses-and-time]])
- **Concentration is risk-*reduction*, not risk-amplification, when paired with deep work.** The 1993 letter inverts the diversification-as-safety framing — depth of knowledge is the substitute for breadth of holdings. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/position-concentration]])
- **Circle of competence — its size doesn't matter; knowing its boundaries does.** The 1996 owner's manual. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1996-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/circle-of-competence]])
- **Intrinsic value is a present-value estimate of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life.** The 1993 letter's compressed definition; the 1989 letter's broader framing of intrinsic-value-vs-book-value. ([[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]], [[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/intrinsic-value]])
- **Net buyers welcome declining prices.** "Declining prices for businesses benefit us, and rising prices hurt us… It's optimism that is the enemy of the rational buyer." ([[sources/buffett/letters/1990-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/drawdowns-and-temperament]])
- **Pessimism is your friend, euphoria the enemy.** Same disposition restated under the 2008 financial crisis. ([[sources/buffett/letters/2008-berkshire-letter]])
- **The Secret Sauce: a few decisions, decades apart, do most of the work.** "Our satisfactory results have been the product of about a dozen truly good decisions — that would be about one every five years." ([[sources/buffett/letters/2022-berkshire-letter]] — see [[topics/position-concentration]])
- **Mistakes fade away; winners can forever blossom.** The asymmetric distribution of investment outcomes restated in the penultimate letter. ([[sources/buffett/letters/2024-berkshire-letter]])

## Characteristic mental models

- The investor as a *would-be private owner* pricing the whole business, not as a trader pricing the next quote
- The 1965 "Generals / Workouts / Controls" taxonomy of investment categories
- The 20-hole punch card as meta-discipline ([[topics/position-concentration]])
- "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful" — the operating posture on [[topics/mr-market-and-temperament]] ([[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]])
- One-foot hurdles, not seven-foot ones — the operational consequence of [[topics/circle-of-competence]] ([[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]])
- "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get." — the Graham-rooted aphorism quoted across multiple Berkshire letters ([[sources/buffett/letters/2008-berkshire-letter]])
- The "owner's manual" voice — the disposition of writing to shareholders as if they were partners, not customers
- *Look-through earnings* (1991 onwards) — the recognition that Berkshire's share of investee retained earnings is economically real even if GAAP excludes it

## Recurring failure modes Buffett warns against

- **The "Cash Flow = (a) + (b)" presentation** that ignores (c) maintenance capex — used by investment-banker salesmen to justify the unjustifiable ([[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]])
- **The academic substitution of volatility for risk** — beta as a measure of investment danger, attacked across the 1993 and 2014 letters ([[sources/buffett/letters/1993-berkshire-letter]])
- **The fee/cost extraction by intermediaries** — repeatedly attacked from the early 1980s onwards; the 1996 letter's index-fund recommendation is the constructive response
- **"This time it's different" extrapolation at cycle peaks** — bubble-style behaviour described in the 2000 letter ("speculation is most dangerous when it looks easiest")
- **Acting from emotion (pique, regret, FOMO)** — the Berkshire takeover is the canonical Buffett-against-himself example ([[sources/buffett/letters/1965-partnership-letter]])
- **Confusing GAAP earnings with owner earnings** — leads to systematically over-paying ([[sources/buffett/letters/1986-berkshire-letter]])
- **Believing managerial brilliance can overcome bad business economics** — "the reputation of the business remains intact" ([[sources/buffett/letters/1989-berkshire-letter]])
- **Forbidding the words "mistake" or "error" in board meetings or analyst calls** — the cultural pre-condition for unforced errors of the kind Buffett tries to flag transparently ([[sources/buffett/letters/2024-berkshire-letter]])
