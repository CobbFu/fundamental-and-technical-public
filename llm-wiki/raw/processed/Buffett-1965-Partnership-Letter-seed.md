## Context

By the end of 1965, Buffett Partnership Ltd. had achieved a cumulative compounded return ahead of the Dow Jones Industrial Average over the partnership's life. The 1965 letter is read today primarily for three things:

1. The taking of operating control of Berkshire Hathaway, a New England textile manufacturer — a decision Buffett would later call a strategic mistake but one of the foundation events of his investing career.
2. The articulation of the categories of investments Buffett pursued — "Generals," "Workouts," and "Controls" — and the reasoning behind each.
3. Buffett's continued emphasis on **margin of safety** and **conservative valuation**, inherited from Benjamin Graham, and his early framing of what would later become his patience-and-temperament philosophy.

## Investment categories

Buffett distinguished:

- **Generals — Private Owner Basis.** Securities selling well below their value to a private owner, where Buffett had no special insight into corporate decisions but priced the company as a whole would-be acquirer might.
- **Generals — Relatively Undervalued.** Securities priced cheap relative to peers, with the expected catalyst being market re-rating rather than corporate action.
- **Workouts.** Securities whose return depended on corporate action — mergers, liquidations, reorganisations — rather than general market direction. Workouts produced steady returns largely uncorrelated with the Dow.
- **Controls.** Situations where the partnership took a large enough stake to influence corporate policy directly. Berkshire Hathaway became a Control in 1965.

## On margin of safety

Buffett returned, as he did across the partnership-era letters, to Graham's central idea: the buyer of a security must demand a *margin of safety* — a meaningful gap between estimated intrinsic value and price paid — to absorb the inevitable errors in estimation that all investors make. Buffett framed this not as a single calculation but as a *posture*: a refusal to act when the margin is thin, even when the price action invites action.

## On Mr. Market and temperament

The Graham allegory of "Mr. Market" — a manic-depressive business partner who shows up daily quoting prices, sometimes wildly high and sometimes wildly low — was a recurring frame across Buffett's partnership letters. The investor's job was not to *predict* Mr. Market's mood but to *exploit* it: accept his prices when they were attractive, ignore them when they were not. This required the temperament to do nothing for long periods.

## On concentration

Buffett's partnership letters foreshadowed his later, more famous "20-hole punch card" formulation: that an investor with only twenty lifetime investment decisions would, by necessity, make better ones. In the 1965 letter the concentration language was less aphoristic but the disposition was already visible — the partnership made few new commitments per year, each well-researched, each sized to materially impact the partnership's returns when right.

## On the Dow as benchmark

Buffett's stated objective was to beat the Dow Jones Industrial Average by an average of ten percentage points per year over the long run. He warned partners — repeatedly — that the partnership would have *down years* and that any year in which the partnership only matched the Dow's positive return would be an unsatisfactory one for him, even if the absolute number was respectable. The benchmark was always relative performance over the long term, not absolute performance over the short.

## On owner earnings (proto)

While Buffett would not formalise the term "owner earnings" until his 1986 chairman's letter, the 1965 partnership letter and its peers already showed the analytical posture: looking past reported GAAP earnings to the cash a business could actually return to its owners without impairing its competitive position. Capital expenditure, working-capital needs, and management's compensation behaviour were already being treated as adjustments to the headline number.

## On the Berkshire takeover

The acquisition of operating control of Berkshire Hathaway was discussed candidly. The textile business was, by 1965, structurally challenged — high capital intensity, low return on invested capital, declining unit demand. Buffett would later reflect that the decision to control Berkshire was an emotional rather than analytical one (he was provoked by a CEO who had offered to tender his shares at a higher price than was eventually delivered). The lesson Buffett would draw, decades later, was that he should never let pique drive a capital allocation decision.

## Widely-cited individual passages

These short passages are individually quoted in many secondary sources and biographies (Lowenstein, Schroeder, Cunningham, Loomis, etc.) and capture Buffett's posture across the partnership letters:

- "The most important quality for an investor is temperament, not intellect."
- "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful." (later, summary of Buffett's repeated framing)
- "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."
- "Our favorite holding period is forever."
- "Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1."
- "You only have to do a very few things right in your life so long as you don't do too many things wrong."

(Attribution note: these aphorisms appear across multiple Buffett letters and shareholder-meeting Q&As; they encapsulate the partnership-era and chairman-era posture rather than belonging exclusively to 1965.)
